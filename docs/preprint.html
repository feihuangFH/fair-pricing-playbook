The Fair Pricing Playbook

A practical framework for developing, evaluating, and auditing fair algorithmic pricing systems

Author

Fei Huang

Published

June 16, 2026

Abstract

Algorithmic pricing is now common in insurance and financial services, but practical guidance for translating fairness principles into deployable workflows remains fragmented. This preprint presents The Fair Pricing Playbook, a four-step framework that links fairness definition, model design, welfare assessment, and post-deployment audit into one end-to-end process. The framework centers the anti-discrimination lens while keeping actuarial fairness, solidarity, and consumer trust visible in governance decisions. Three technical case studies illustrate implementation choices and trade-offs in insurance settings. The goal is operational clarity: how to choose a fairness criterion, map it to modeling interventions, evaluate market consequences, and run statistically valid compliance audits.

Keywords

Algorithmic pricing, insurance, fairness, anti-discrimination, welfare analysis, audit, responsible AI.

1. Introduction

AI-driven pricing systems are now widely used in insurance and other financial services. Their appeal is clear: better risk segmentation, faster decisions, and potentially better matching between risk and price. At the same time, these systems can reproduce or amplify discriminatory patterns through proxy variables, complex model interactions, and opaque optimization pipelines. This creates a governance gap between predictive performance and fairness accountability.

In regulated settings, fairness is not a single model constraint. It is a sequence of decisions spanning criterion selection, model design, market behavior, and post-deployment testing (Frees and Huang 2023; Xin and Huang 2024). A model can satisfy one fairness notion and still produce adverse outcomes once final prices incorporate loadings, demand response, and regulatory rules (Huang, Shimao, and Khern-am-nuai 2026; Huang and Shimao 2026). Likewise, an audit can fail if statistical inference is mis-specified even when design intent is sound (Huang and Hooker 2026).

This preprint introduces The Fair Pricing Playbook, a practical four-step framework that links these decisions into one auditable workflow.

2. Related Literature and Positioning

This work builds on three main streams:

  1. Insurance fairness principles and regulation. Prior work clarifies anti-discrimination obligations, distinctions between risk-based and non-risk differentiation, and jurisdictional variation in acceptable rating factors (Frees and Huang 2023; Xin and Huang 2024).
  2. Welfare and market effects of fairness interventions. Recent studies show that fairness at the cost-model stage does not mechanically translate into fairness in final prices, markups, or consumer welfare (Huang, Shimao, and Khern-am-nuai 2026; Huang and Shimao 2026).
  3. Audit methodology for algorithmic pricing. Emerging audit protocols emphasize pre-commitment, corrected inference for deterministic pricing outputs, and equivalence-based decision logic (Huang and Hooker 2026; Xin, Hooker, and Huang 2026).

Compared with these contributions, the playbook’s role is integrative: it provides an operational bridge from policy framing to modeling choices, welfare analysis, and audit execution.

3. Framework Overview

The framework has four linked steps:

  1. Define fairness. Establish fairness lens, legal context, and criterion (for example FTU, CPV, DP, CDP), and document legitimate factors.
  2. Design fair pricing. Map criterion to intervention stage (pre-, in-, post-processing) and model class (M0, MU, MDP, MCDP, MC) (Xin and Huang 2024).
  3. Assess impact. Evaluate final-price outcomes under demand, regulation, and market response, including firm profit and consumer welfare (Huang and Shimao 2026).
  4. Audit the system. Apply pre-committed tests (for example CDP and PD) with corrected standard errors and explicit pass/fail/insufficient-information rules (Huang and Hooker 2026).

The logic is sequential. Later-stage evaluation and audit should be aligned with earlier-stage fairness commitments, and protocol choices should be fixed before reviewing outcomes.

4. Methodological Core by Step

4.1 Step 1: Define fairness criteria and governance scope

Step 1 anchors the framework in anti-discrimination while keeping other fairness lenses visible (actuarial fairness, solidarity, consumer trust). It distinguishes individual and group fairness criteria and emphasizes that criteria are illustrative rather than exhaustive (Krafcheck, Balnozan, and Huang 2026).

4.2 Step 2: Translate criteria into model interventions

Step 2 formalizes how criteria map to model interventions:

  • Pre-processing: debias selected predictors before fitting (MDP/MCDP).
  • In-processing: include fairness constraints directly in training or optimization objectives.
  • Post-processing: adjust prediction outputs while preserving baseline model structure (MC).

This step focuses on fair cost modeling (pure premium / expected claim cost) as a technical foundation for downstream pricing decisions.

4.3 Step 3: Evaluate welfare implications at final-price stage

Step 3 extends analysis from predicted cost to final prices under pricing rules and demand response. It evaluates fairness trade-offs jointly with welfare and profit. This addresses an important gap: fairness properties may change once prices are optimized or constrained by policy rules (Huang, Shimao, and Khern-am-nuai 2026; Huang and Shimao 2026).

4.4 Step 4: Audit with corrected inference

Step 4 operationalizes compliance testing using regression-based audits and equivalence logic. For CDP-style audits, TOST frames the decision as demonstrating compliance within tolerance, rather than merely failing to reject a zero-gap null. The framework distinguishes pass, fail, and insufficient information outcomes and prescribes pre-specified escalation pathways (Huang and Hooker 2026).

5. Companion Evidence from Case Studies

Three companion case studies provide technical evidence and implementation detail:

  • Case Study 1: cost-model fairness interventions under alternative criteria and model classes.
  • Case Study 2: welfare and pricing-rule trade-offs across premium, markup, and profit outcomes.
  • Case Study 3: deployment-stage fairness testing with corrected inference and protocol-based decision rules.

The studies are intended as reproducible illustrations, not universal regulatory templates. Data, tolerance bands, and admissible factors remain jurisdiction- and product-specific.

6. Contributions

This preprint contributes:

  • a practical governance-to-model-to-audit workflow for fair algorithmic pricing;
  • explicit mapping between fairness criteria and intervention design;
  • integration of welfare analysis with fairness assessment;
  • an auditable testing protocol with transparent outcome classification.

7. Limitations and Scope

The framework is designed for operational clarity, not legal determination. It does not claim one globally valid criterion or one approved model pipeline. Legal burden, tolerated disparity, and acceptable predictors vary across markets. The playbook should be interpreted as a structured decision framework to support policy, actuarial, and technical coordination.

8. Conclusion

Fair algorithmic pricing requires more than model-level constraints. It requires coherent choices across definition, design, market impact, and audit. The Fair Pricing Playbook offers a practical, citable framework for that full pipeline and supports adaptation to local regulatory and product contexts.
