The Fair Pricing Playbook

A practical framework for developing, evaluating, and auditing fair algorithmic pricing systems

Author
Affiliation

Fei Huang

School of Risk and Actuarial Studies, UNSW Business School, UNSW Sydney

Published

June 16, 2026

Keywords

algorithmic pricing, insurance, fairness, anti-discrimination, welfare analysis, audit, responsible AI

Abstract

Algorithmic pricing is now common in insurance and financial services, but practical guidance for translating fairness principles into deployable workflows remains fragmented. This preprint presents The Fair Pricing Playbook, a four-step framework that links fairness definition, model design, welfare assessment, and post-deployment audit into one end-to-end process. The framework centers the anti-discrimination lens while keeping actuarial fairness, solidarity, and consumer trust visible in governance decisions. Three technical case studies illustrate implementation choices and trade-offs in insurance settings: fair cost-model design across competing fairness criteria and model classes; welfare and pricing-rule analysis under demand response and regulation; and deployment-stage statistical testing with corrected inference. The goal is operational clarity: how to choose a fairness criterion, map it to modeling interventions, evaluate market consequences, and run statistically valid compliance audits.

Keywords: algorithmic pricing, insurance, fairness, anti-discrimination, welfare analysis, audit, responsible AI.

1. Introduction

AI-driven pricing systems are now widely used in insurance and other financial services. Their appeal is clear: better risk segmentation, faster decisions, and potentially better matching between risk and price. At the same time, these systems can reproduce or amplify discriminatory patterns through proxy variables, complex model interactions, and opaque optimization pipelines. This creates a governance gap between predictive performance and fairness accountability.

In regulated settings, fairness is not a single model constraint. It is a sequence of decisions spanning criterion selection, model design, market behavior, and post-deployment testing (Frees and Huang 2023; Xin and Huang 2024). A model can satisfy one fairness notion and still produce adverse outcomes once final prices incorporate loadings, demand response, and regulatory rules (Huang, Shimao, and Khern-am-nuai 2026; Huang and Shimao 2026). Likewise, an audit can fail if statistical inference is mis-specified even when design intent is sound (Huang and Hooker 2026).

This preprint introduces The Fair Pricing Playbook, a practical four-step framework that links these decisions into one auditable workflow. The full playbook, including interactive case studies and implementation checklists, is available at fair.feihuang.org. This preprint provides a self-contained, citable description of the framework, its methodological foundations, and the evidence supplied by the companion case studies.

2. Related Literature and Positioning

This work builds on three main streams:

  1. Insurance fairness principles and regulation. Prior work clarifies anti-discrimination obligations, distinctions between risk-based and non-risk differentiation, and jurisdictional variation in acceptable rating factors (Frees and Huang 2023; Xin and Huang 2024; Krafcheck, Balnozan, and Huang 2026).
  2. Welfare and market effects of fairness interventions. Recent studies show that fairness at the cost-model stage does not mechanically translate into fairness in final prices, markups, or consumer welfare (Huang, Shimao, and Khern-am-nuai 2026; Huang and Shimao 2026).
  3. Audit methodology for algorithmic pricing. Emerging audit protocols emphasize pre-commitment, corrected inference for deterministic pricing outputs, and equivalence-based decision logic (Huang and Hooker 2026; Xin, Hooker, and Huang 2026).

Compared with these contributions, the playbook’s role is integrative: it provides an operational bridge from policy framing to modeling choices, welfare analysis, and audit execution. It is designed to be used by practitioners across compliance, actuarial, data science, and governance functions and is not a legal determination of what any jurisdiction requires.

3. Framework Overview

The framework has four linked steps, summarized in Table 1.

Table 1. The four steps of the Fair Pricing Playbook.
Step Topic Governing question
1 Define fairness What does fair mean in this context?
2 Design fair pricing How can the chosen criterion be achieved?
3 Assess impact Who gains and loses after prices are set?
4 Audit the system Does the deployed system pass or fail?

The logic is sequential. Step 1 fixes the fairness criterion and governance scope before any modeling begins. Step 2 translates that criterion into a model intervention - pre-, in-, or post-processing. Step 3 evaluates whether fairness at the cost-model stage carries through to final prices under demand, regulation, and market response. Step 4 applies pre-committed statistical tests to deployed pricing outputs using inference methods suited to deterministic algorithmic outputs.

Later-stage evaluation and audit should be aligned with earlier-stage fairness commitments, and all protocol choices should be fixed before reviewing outcomes. Post-hoc changes to criteria, tolerance margins, or sample definitions undermine auditability and error-rate control.

4. Methodological Core by Step

4.1 Step 1: Define fairness criteria and governance scope

Step 1 anchors the framework in the anti-discrimination lens while keeping other fairness perspectives - actuarial fairness, solidarity, and consumer trust - visible in governance decisions. It distinguishes individual and group fairness criteria, including fairness through unawareness (FTU), demographic parity (DP), conditional demographic parity (CDP), and controlling for the protected variable (CPV), and emphasizes that criteria are illustrative rather than exhaustive (Krafcheck, Balnozan, and Huang 2026). Legitimate rating factors - those recognized by the applicable regulator as acceptable bases for price differentiation - are documented at this stage and carry forward into Steps 2 and 4.

4.2 Step 2: Translate criteria into model interventions

Step 2 formalizes how fairness criteria map to model interventions at the cost-modeling stage (pure premium or expected claim cost). Following the notation and model taxonomy of Xin and Huang (2024), interventions are classified by pipeline stage:

  • Pre-processing: debiased predictors are constructed before model fitting. Models MDP (demographic parity) and MCDP (conditional demographic parity) fit on transformed non-protected predictors X^\star_{NP} so that average predicted outcomes are approximately equal across protected groups, or equal within strata defined by legitimate factors.
  • In-processing: fairness constraints are incorporated directly into the training or optimization objective.
  • Post-processing: a full model (M0) is fitted, then predictions are averaged over protected-group membership at scoring time (model MC, targeting CPV).

The baseline model M0 - which uses all variables including protected attributes - is retained for welfare and audit comparisons in Steps 3 and 4, not for deployment where discrimination is prohibited. Model MU (unawareness) trains without the protected attribute as a simple FTU baseline.

4.3 Step 3: Evaluate welfare implications at the final-price stage

Step 3 extends analysis from predicted cost to final prices under pricing rules and demand response. A fair cost model does not guarantee a fair or welfare-improving final price: fairness properties can change once prices are optimized, constrained by regulation, or filtered through market participation and selection effects (Huang, Shimao, and Khern-am-nuai 2026; Huang and Shimao 2026).

The step evaluates five illustrative pricing rules - unconstrained profit maximization (P0), accountable pricing (PA), price-optimization bans (POB), demographic parity on premiums (PDP), and group fairness on cost estimates (PAF) - across consumer welfare, firm profit, and markup gaps by protected group. Key findings from the companion research are that price fairness and markup fairness are in tension: no single rule achieves both. PDP closes premium gaps but can widen markup disparities. PA reduces markup gaps at a cost in firm profit. The effect of POB depends on whether participation is mandatory or voluntary.

Welfare assessment uses consumer surplus (willingness to pay minus price paid) and firm profit (price minus expected cost), reported by protected group. Step 3 findings inform the monitoring plan that is pre-committed in Step 4.

4.4 Step 4: Audit with corrected inference

Step 4 operationalizes compliance testing using a pre-committed Plan -> Audit -> Decide -> Improve protocol (Huang and Hooker 2026). All design choices - fairness criterion, protected attribute (or proxy inference method), legitimate rating factors, response variable, tolerance margins, and sample design - are fixed before examining pricing data. Post-hoc modifications to any of these choices undermine stated error-rate control.

The statistical core addresses a key challenge: deterministic algorithmic pricing outputs produce residuals that reflect model approximation error, not sampling variability, so classical OLS standard errors are unreliable. The framework prescribes heteroskedasticity-consistent (HC3) standard errors for CDP audits and cross-covariance correction for proxy-discrimination (PD) audits.

Decision logic uses equivalence testing (TOST) for CDP: compliance is established only when statistical evidence shows the price gap falls within a pre-specified tolerance band, not merely when a zero-gap null fails to be rejected. This produces three explicit outcomes - pass, fail, and insufficient information - with pre-specified escalation pathways for each. Proxy-imputed protected attributes (BISG, BIFSG, or zip-code composition) are accommodated with documented limitations.

5. Companion Evidence from Case Studies

Three case studies provide technical evidence and implementation detail. They are intended as reproducible illustrations, not universal regulatory templates. Data, tolerance bands, and admissible factors remain jurisdiction- and product-specific.

Case Study 1: Fair cost-model design

Case Study 1 implements the model taxonomy from Step 2 on insurance claim data using generalized linear models (GLMs) and gradient-boosted trees (XGBoost). It applies pre-processing (MDP, MCDP) and post-processing (MC) interventions under FTU, DP, CDP, and CPV criteria and compares predictive accuracy and cost-model fairness across model classes. Key illustrations include the construction of debiased predictors X^\star_{NP}, the accuracy-fairness trade-off at the cost-model stage, and the dependence of intervention choice on which criterion is binding under local regulation. The study shows that no single model dominates across all criteria and that the appropriate design choice requires knowing the regulatory obligation first.

Case Study 2: Welfare and pricing-rule trade-offs

Case Study 2 implements the welfare analysis pipeline from Step 3 on insurance market data. It incorporates demand modeling (price elasticity by group), price optimization under each of the five pricing rules (P0, PA, POB, PDP, PAF), and computes consumer welfare and firm profit by protected group under each rule. The study confirms that fairness interventions at the cost-model stage do not translate uniformly into welfare improvements at the market stage: some rules that equalize premiums increase markup disparity, and selection effects under voluntary participation can reverse the intended direction of a fairness intervention. Findings illustrate why welfare assessment is a necessary complement to cost-model fairness evaluation.

Case Study 3: Deployment-stage fairness testing

Case Study 3 implements the audit protocol from Step 4 on auto insurance quote data from Illinois. It demonstrates CDP and PD audits with HC3-corrected standard errors, showing concretely how classical OLS inference overstates precision for deterministic pricing outputs. The study applies TOST-based equivalence testing for CDP with 5% price-gap and 0.80 adverse-impact-ratio tolerance margins, producing explicit pass, fail, and insufficient-information verdicts. It also implements a proxy-discrimination test for a suspect rating variable, illustrating how a variable’s effect changes when a proxied protected attribute enters the regression. The case study serves as a worked example of the full pre-committed audit workflow.

6. Contributions

This preprint contributes:

  • a practical governance-to-model-to-audit workflow for fair algorithmic pricing, linking four decision stages into one coherent and auditable pipeline;
  • explicit mapping between fairness criteria (FTU, DP, CDP, CPV) and intervention design (pre-, in-, post-processing), with a comparative model taxonomy;
  • integration of welfare analysis - firm profit and consumer surplus by protected group - with cost-model fairness assessment;
  • an auditable testing protocol with corrected statistical inference, equivalence-based decision logic, and transparent three-way outcome classification (pass / fail / insufficient information);
  • open access implementation via a companion website at fair.feihuang.org, with reproducible case studies in insurance.

7. Limitations and Scope

The framework is designed for operational clarity, not legal determination. It does not prescribe one globally valid fairness criterion, one approved model pipeline, or one set of acceptable rating factors. Legal burden, tolerated disparity, and admissible predictors vary substantially across markets and product lines. Regulatory proposals in jurisdictions such as Colorado and New York have operationalized CDP-style and proxy-discrimination tests in ways that partially overlap with Step 4, but the playbook should not be treated as a compliance safe harbor in any specific jurisdiction.

The welfare analysis in Step 3 relies on demand modeling assumptions (price elasticity, participation structure) that must be calibrated to the specific product and market. The audit protocol in Step 4 requires pre-specification and is not designed for exploratory analysis of existing pricing data.

The playbook should be interpreted as a structured decision framework to support policy, actuarial, and technical coordination - a starting point for organizations designing fair pricing governance, not an endpoint.

8. Conclusion

Fair algorithmic pricing requires more than model-level constraints. It requires coherent choices across fairness definition, model design, market impact, and audit methodology. The Fair Pricing Playbook offers a practical, citable framework for that full pipeline and supports adaptation to local regulatory and product contexts. By linking criterion selection to model intervention, welfare analysis, and pre-committed audit protocols, it provides a workflow that is defensible in governance review and actionable in practice.
